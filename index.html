What IS a Business?

Before you start, let's learn the basics every entrepreneur needs to know.

üí°

A Business Solves a Problem

A business is when you sell a product (something people can hold) or a service (something you do for people) to earn money. Every good business starts by finding a problem people have and offering a solution.

Example

Students are always losing pencils ‚Üí Sell pencil packs at school!

üîÑ

How a Business Makes Money

You spend money to create or buy something (that's your cost), then sell it for more than you spent. The difference is your profit. It's that simple!

The Formula

Revenue (money coming in) ‚àí Costs (money going out) = Profit üí∞

üéØ

Know Your Customer

Your customer is the person who buys from you. You need to know what they want, how much they'll pay, and where to find them. For this game, your customers are your classmates!

Ask yourself

Would my friends actually spend their own money on this?

‚öñÔ∏è

Supply & Demand

If everyone wants something but nobody's selling it, you can charge more (high demand, low supply). If lots of people sell the same thing, you need to stand out or lower your price.

Think about it

If 5 students sell slime, but nobody sells bookmarks, which has less competition?

ü§ù

What is an Investor?

An investor gives you money to help start or grow your business. In return, they get a percentage of your profits. They're taking a risk ‚Äî if your business fails, they lose their money too!

Example Deal

A Shark invests $5 for 25% of profits. If you earn $20 profit, the Shark gets $5 back.

üìà

Why Businesses Fail

Most businesses fail because they don't plan, they spend too much, they price wrong, or nobody actually wants what they're selling. That's why research and planning matter!

Top reason for failure

Making something YOU like instead of something your CUSTOMERS want.

1

Find a Problem to Solve

Look around! What do your classmates need? What do they complain about? What would make their day easier or more fun? That's where business ideas come from.

üí° Tip: Ask 5 friends "What do you wish you could buy at school?" Their answers = your business idea!
2

Do Your Research

Before spending money, find out if people will actually buy your product. Ask classmates if they'd buy it and how much they'd pay. Check if anyone else is already selling something similar.

üí° Tip: Create a quick 3-question survey and ask 10 people. That's real market research!
3

Make a Plan

Write down: What will you sell? Who will buy it? How much does it cost to make? How much will you charge? Where will you sell it? This is your business plan!

üí° Tip: Use the "Build Your Pitch" section below to create your plan!
4

Calculate ALL Your Costs

List everything you need to buy: supplies, materials, packaging, labels. Add it all up. This is your startup cost. Remember ‚Äî in this game it must be between $1 and $10!

üí° Tip: Don't forget hidden costs like tape, bags, or markers for signs!
5

Set Your Price

Your price must be MORE than what each item costs you to make. Charge at least 2√ó your cost per item. Too high? Nobody buys. Too low? You don't make money.

üí° Tip: If one bracelet costs you $0.50 to make, sell it for at least $1.00‚Äì$2.00!
6

Start Small ‚Äî Test First!

Don't make 100 items right away. Make 5‚Äì10, sell them, see what happens. Listen to feedback. Then improve and make more. This is called a "pilot" or test run.

üí° Tip: Starting small means if something doesn't work, you haven't wasted all your money!
7

Market Your Business

Tell people about your product! Make flyers, tell friends, create a catchy business name. Word of mouth is the #1 way students sell. Make it easy for people to find you.

üí° Tip: Offer a "first customer discount" or a "buy 2 get 1 free" deal to get people talking!
8

Sell & Be Professional

Have your products ready and looking good. Be friendly, give correct change, and thank every customer. Happy customers tell their friends = more sales for you!

üí° Tip: Presentation matters! A cookie in a nice bag sells better than one in a plain napkin.
9

Track Every Dollar

Write down every sale (money in) and every expense (money out). At the end, subtract costs from sales. Positive number = profit! Negative = loss. Use the tracker below.

üí° Tip: Real business owners do this every day. It's called "bookkeeping."
10

Grow or Pivot

Working great? Make more and expand! Not selling? Change your approach ‚Äî new price, new product, new audience. Smart businesses adapt. Failing isn't bad ‚Äî quitting without trying is!

üí° Tip: Many famous companies started by selling something completely different than what they sell now!
üíµ

Revenue

Revenue is ALL the money your business brings in from sales. If you sell 20 bracelets at $2 each, your revenue is $40. But that's NOT your profit yet!

Formula

Revenue = Price √ó Quantity Sold
$2 √ó 20 bracelets = $40 revenue

üìâ

Costs / Expenses

Costs are everything you spend to run your business: supplies, materials, packaging. Subtract these from your revenue to find your real profit.

Example costs

Beads $3 + String $2 + Bags $1 = $6 total costs

üèÜ

Profit

Profit is what's LEFT after you subtract ALL costs from your revenue. This is YOUR money to keep (or share with investors)!

The Math

$40 revenue ‚àí $6 costs = $34 profit! üéâ

üìä

Markup

Markup is how much you add to your cost to set your selling price. If something costs you $0.50 and you sell it for $2.00, your markup is $1.50 (or 300%).

Rule of thumb

Charge at least 2√ó your cost for a healthy profit margin.

‚öñÔ∏è

Break Even

Break even is when your revenue EQUALS your costs. You haven't made a profit yet, but you haven't lost money either. Everything after this point is profit!

Example

You spent $6. Each bracelet earns $2. After selling 3 bracelets ($6), you break even. Bracelet #4 is pure profit!

ü§ù

How Investors Profit

When a Shark invests in your business, you agree on a deal. Usually, the investor gets a percentage of your profits. The bigger the investment, the bigger their share.

Example Deal

Shark invests $3 for 30% of profits.
You earn $34 profit ‚Üí Shark gets $10.20, you keep $23.80 ü§ù

!

‚ùå Spending All Your Money at Once

Don't blow your entire budget on supplies before testing if anyone wants your product. Start with a small test batch. If it sells, buy more!

!

‚ùå Pricing Too Low

If you charge too little, you might sell a lot but still lose money! Always make sure your selling price is at least double your cost per item.

!

‚ùå Copying Everyone Else

If 5 people are already selling slime, don't be person #6! Find something unique or put a creative twist on an existing idea to stand out.

!

‚ùå Not Tracking Your Money

If you don't write down your sales and expenses, you won't know if you're making money or losing it. Keep a simple log of everything!

!

‚ùå Ignoring Customer Feedback

If customers say "it's too expensive" or "I wish it came in blue," LISTEN! Adjusting based on feedback is how smart businesses grow.

!

‚ùå Giving Up Too Fast

Your first day might be slow. That's normal! Adjust your strategy, try new marketing, and keep going. Most successful entrepreneurs failed before they succeeded.

The 5 Questions Every Business Must Answer

Whether you're pitching or investing, EVERY business must answer these 5 questions. If they can't, it's a red flag! üö©

01

What Is Your Business?

Describe exactly what you're selling in one clear sentence. Is it a product (something you sell) or a service (something you do for people)? What makes it special?

Strong Answer

"I make custom friendship bracelets with school colors and names using beads and string."

02

Who Will Buy It?

Who is your target customer? Be specific! "Everyone" is not a good answer. Think about who would actually pull out their wallet and buy your product.

Strong Answer

"6th‚Äì8th grade girls who like jewelry and want to match with their friend groups. Especially popular before dances and spirit weeks."

03

What Does It Cost to Start?

List every single thing you need to buy and the total startup cost. Investors want to know exactly where their money is going. Be honest and detailed!

Strong Answer

"Beads $3, string $1.50, clasps $1, small bags $1 = $6.50 total startup cost. This makes about 25 bracelets."

04

What's Your Selling Price?

How much will you charge per item? Explain why that price is fair. Show the math: cost per item, selling price, and profit per item. Sharks love numbers!

Strong Answer

"Each bracelet costs me $0.26 to make. I'll sell them for $1.50 each. That's $1.24 profit per bracelet, or a 477% markup!"

05

Why Will It Succeed?

What's your plan? How will people find out about your product? Why will they choose YOU over someone else? What's your advantage? This is where confidence and creativity matter!

Strong Answer

"Nobody else at school sells custom jewelry. I'll take orders at lunch, offer free color matching, and give a 'buy 3 get 1 free' deal for friend groups."

ü¶à Bonus Question for Sharks: How Do Investors Profit?

When you invest money in someone's business, you agree on a deal. Typically the entrepreneur offers a percentage of their profits in exchange for the investment. Before investing, do the math:

Example Deal: You invest $3 for 25% of profits
If they profit $20: You get $5 back (25% of $20)
Your ROI: Invested $3, got $5 = $2 profit! üìà

üè´ For the Teacher

How to Run the Game in Class

Follow this timeline to run a smooth, fun, and educational Shark Tank session!

Introduce the Game

‚è± 5‚Äì10 minutes

Explain the rules: each student gets a set amount of cash ($1‚Äì$10). They choose to be an Entrepreneur or a Shark. Hand out the money and let students explore the business ideas section.

Teach the Basics

‚è± 10‚Äì15 minutes

Walk through the "What IS a Business?" and "5 Key Questions" sections as a class. Make sure every student understands revenue, costs, and profit before they start planning.

Entrepreneurs Plan Their Pitch

‚è± 15‚Äì20 minutes

Entrepreneurs use the "Build Your Pitch" section or sample pitch cards to create their presentation. They must answer all 5 key questions and have real numbers. Sharks can review the evaluation checklist.

Shark Tank Presentations!

‚è± 2‚Äì3 minutes per pitch

Each Entrepreneur stands up and presents their pitch. Sharks listen, take notes on their evaluation scorecards, and can ask 2‚Äì3 questions after each pitch.

Sharks Make Deals

‚è± 10 minutes

After all pitches, Sharks decide who to invest in. They physically hand their cash to the Entrepreneurs they believe in and agree on the profit-sharing percentage.

Run the Businesses (Optional Extended Play)

‚è± 1‚Äì5 days

If doing an extended game, Entrepreneurs actually create and sell their products over the week. They track money using the tracker. At the end, they calculate profits and pay investors.

Final Results & Awards

‚è± 10 minutes

Announce results! Awards: Most Profitable Business, Best Pitch, Smartest Shark (highest ROI), Most Creative Business, Best Teamwork, and Comeback Award (best pivot from failure).
